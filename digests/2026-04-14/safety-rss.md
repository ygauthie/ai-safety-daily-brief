# Blog Posts & Articles (2026-04-14)

## Top Stories

**[Anthropic repeatedly accidentally trained against the CoT, demonstrating inadequate processes](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/K8FxfK9GmJfiAhgcT/anthropic-repeatedly-accidentally-trained-against-the-cot)** - A critical safety failure has been revealed at Anthropic, where the company accidentally trained against Claude Mythos Preview's chain of thought (CoT) reasoning in approximately 8% of training episodes. This represents at least the second independent incident where Anthropic unintentionally exposed their model's internal reasoning to the oversight signal. The post argues this demonstrates inadequate development processes that could prove catastrophic as AI systems become more powerful and approach superintelligence levels.

**[Summary: AI Governance to Avoid Extinction](https://intelligence.org/2026/04/13/summary-ai-governance-to-avoid-extinction/)** - MIRI has published a summary of their research paper examining governance approaches for managing extinction risks from AI systems. As capabilities rapidly increase toward human-expert-level performance across all domains, the work explores how the world should respond to these developments and identifies actionable research questions for governance frameworks.

**[Import AI 453: Breaking AI agents; MirrorCode; and ten views on gradual disempowerment](https://importai.substack.com/p/import-ai-453-breaking-ai-agents)** - Jack Clark's latest newsletter covers research on AI agent vulnerabilities, the MirrorCode project, and presents ten different perspectives on gradual human disempowerment scenarios as AI systems become more capable.

## Key Arguments

**Process Failures Threaten Safety at Scale** - The Anthropic CoT training incident highlights a fundamental argument about AI safety: that procedural failures in current systems could become existentially dangerous as capabilities scale. The post emphasizes that "in more powerful systems, this kind of failure would jeopardize safely navigating the intelligence explosion," arguing that robust development processes are crucial before human oversight becomes inadequate.

**Legal Frameworks as Last Resort for Extinction Prevention** - The post [Only Law Can Prevent Extinction](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/5CfBDiQNg9upfipWk/only-law-can-prevent-extinction) makes the case that regulatory and legal mechanisms may be the only viable path to preventing AI-related extinction risks, drawing parallels to how government authority ultimately rests on enforcement mechanisms.

**Methodological Clarity in Safety Research** - [Meaningful Questions Have Return Types](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/emsDJNmxBu8Tt6PHt/meaningful-questions-have-return-types) argues for more rigorous framing of AI safety questions, suggesting that progress stalls when researchers ask "wrong questions" that don't align with how reality works - a classic rationalist approach to avoiding conceptual confusion in safety research.

## Community Discussion

The Anthropic CoT incident has sparked significant discussion across both the Alignment Forum and LessWrong communities about the adequacy of current AI safety practices at leading labs. The cross-posting of this content suggests broad concern about whether major AI companies have sufficiently robust processes to prevent dangerous training failures.

There appears to be ongoing debate about governance approaches, with MIRI's governance research summary coinciding with arguments about legal enforcement mechanisms, suggesting the community is actively grappling with both technical and institutional approaches to managing AI risks as capabilities continue to advance rapidly.
