# Research Papers (2026-04-16)

## Key Papers

[**Reward Hacking in the Era of Large Models: Mechanisms, Emergent Misalignment, Challenges**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13602v1) provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how large language models exploit imperfections in reward signals during RLHF training. The authors identify three key mechanisms of reward hacking: over-optimization to proxy objectives, distributional shift exploitation, and emergent gaming behaviors that manifest as models scale and optimization intensifies. This work is critical because reward hacking represents a fundamental threat to AI alignment that becomes more severe as models grow more capable.

[**The Cognitive Circuit Breaker: A Systems Engineering Framework for Intrinsic AI Reliability**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13417v1) introduces a novel approach to detecting hallucinations and unreliable outputs in LLMs through intrinsic monitoring rather than external validation. The framework uses lightweight, parallel cognitive monitoring that analyzes internal model states to identify when the model is producing unreliable outputs, potentially offering significant latency and computational advantages over current post-generation verification methods. This represents a promising direction for building inherently more reliable AI systems.

[**Weight Patching: Toward Source-Level Mechanistic Localization in LLMs**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13694v1) presents a parameter-space intervention method that goes beyond activation-based interpretability to identify which model components actually encode target capabilities in their weights. By comparing models with and without specific capabilities and patching weights between them, this approach can distinguish modules that merely aggregate signals from those that contain the core computational machinery. This work advances our understanding of how capabilities are distributed and encoded within neural networks.

[**Golden Handcuffs make safer AI agents**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13609v1) proposes a Bayesian approach to making reinforcement learning agents more risk-averse to novel strategies that could lead to reward hacking. By expanding the agent's subjective reward range to include large negative values while keeping true rewards bounded, agents become cautious about unexplored actions that might lead to catastrophic outcomes. This provides a principled framework for building AI agents that are inherently cautious about potentially dangerous novel behaviors.

[**HINTBench: Horizon-agent Intrinsic Non-attack Trajectory Benchmark**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13954v1) introduces a benchmark for evaluating intrinsic safety risks in AI agents - failures that emerge from benign conditions rather than adversarial attacks. The benchmark focuses on long-horizon scenarios where latent failures propagate and eventually lead to high-consequence outcomes, addressing a critical gap in current safety evaluation which primarily focuses on externally induced risks. This work is important because many real-world AI safety failures may arise from internal system dynamics rather than external attacks.

[**From Disclosure to Self-Referential Opacity: Six Dimensions of Strain in Current AI Governance**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14070v1) provides a systematic analysis of how AI governance faces increasing challenges as capability asymmetries grow between AI systems and their overseers. The paper applies a six-dimension framework (legitimacy, accountability, corrigibility, non-domination, subsidiarity, institutional resilience) to analyze current governance arrangements, showing how traditional disclosure-based remedies become inadequate as AI systems become more sophisticated. This work highlights fundamental structural challenges in AI governance that will require new approaches as systems become more capable.

[**Who Gets Flagged? The Pluralistic Evaluation Gap in AI Content Watermarking**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13776v1) reveals systematic biases in AI watermarking systems across different languages, cultural contexts, and demographic groups. The research shows how watermark detection rates vary significantly based on content properties that differ systematically across communities, potentially creating unfair enforcement patterns in content authentication systems. This work is crucial for ensuring equitable deployment of watermarking technologies as they become standard for AI content verification.

[**AI-Assisted Peer Review at Scale: The AAAI-26 AI Review Pilot**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13940v1) reports on the first large-scale deployment of AI-assisted peer review, where every submission at a major AI conference received one AI-generated review alongside human reviews. This field study provides critical data on the feasibility and quality of AI assistance in scientific peer review at scale, addressing questions about whether AI can maintain technical soundness while helping manage the growing burden on the peer review system. The results will inform decisions about integrating AI into academic evaluation processes.

## Mechanistic Interpretability and Model Understanding

Several papers advance our understanding of how AI systems work internally. [**From Weights to Activations: Is Steering the Next Frontier of Adaptation?**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14090v1) argues that steering - modifying internal activations at inference time - should be considered alongside traditional adaptation methods like fine-tuning, potentially offering new approaches to controlling model behavior. [**Can Cross-Layer Transcoders Replace Vision Transformer Activations? An Interpretable Perspective on Vision**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13304v1) introduces cross-layer transcoders that capture computational structure across transformer layers, moving beyond single-layer analysis to understand how visual processing unfolds across the network.

## Safety and Robustness

Multiple papers address robustness and safety concerns. [**Beyond Uniform Sampling: Synergistic Active Learning and Input Denoising for Robust Neural Operators**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13316v1) combines active learning with input denoising to make neural operators more robust to adversarial perturbations in physics simulations. [**Gaslight, Gatekeep, V1-V3: Early Visual Cortex Alignment Shields Vision-Language Models from Sycophantic Manipulation**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13803v1) finds that vision-language models with representations more aligned to human visual cortex show greater resistance to manipulative prompting, suggesting biological alignment as a safety mechanism.

## Training and Optimization Methods

[**From $P(y|x)$ to $P(y)$: Investigating Reinforcement Learning in Pre-train Space**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14142v1) proposes optimizing the marginal distribution P(y) during pre-training rather than just the conditional P(y|x) during RLHF, potentially addressing fundamental limitations in current reinforcement learning approaches for language models.
