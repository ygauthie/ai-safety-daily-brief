# Blogs & News (2026-04-28)

## Top Stories

The [UK AI Safety Institute released new research](https://www.aisi.gov.uk/research/evaluating-whether-ai-models-would-sabotage-ai-safety-research) evaluating whether AI models would sabotage AI safety research efforts. While details remain limited from the initial publication, this represents a direct empirical investigation into one of the key alignment risks - whether advanced AI systems might actively work against safety measures designed to constrain them. This matters because it provides concrete empirical grounding for theoretical concerns about AI systems that might resist safety research or oversight.

[Le Monde Diplomatique reported](https://mondediplo.com/2026/05/12anthropic) that Anthropic signed a $200 million Pentagon contract for language model deployment, integrated Claude with Palantir, and operates on Amazon's secret infrastructure despite its public safety-focused positioning. The report suggests political pressure from the White House and Pentagon guidance are reshaping how large language models are integrated into U.S. defense contracts. This matters because it highlights potential tensions between AI safety commitments and national security applications that could influence how safety-conscious companies develop and deploy their systems.

Zvi Mowshowitz published an analysis of [OpenAI's newly announced GPT-5.5 system card](https://thezvi.substack.com/p/gpt-55-the-system-card), following last week's release of GPT-5.5 and GPT-5.5-Pro. His review examines the safety evaluations, capability assessments, and risk mitigation strategies documented in OpenAI's system card for this latest generation of models. This matters because system cards provide crucial transparency into how leading labs are evaluating and managing risks from increasingly capable AI systems.

A new [Alignment Forum post](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/cmaJ76Sy9DfZ4EHaZ/language-models-know-what-matters-and-the-foundations-of) argues provocatively that "Language models know what matters and the foundations of ethics better than you," presenting examples of AI systems demonstrating sophisticated moral reasoning, particularly highlighting interactions with Gemini 3. The post includes extensive input-output examples to support claims about AI moral competence. This matters because it challenges assumptions about AI moral reasoning capabilities and could influence how we think about AI systems' roles in ethical decision-making.

Researchers published a [replication study of the Sleeper Agents work](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/mu7eJdesBkKuBycnY/sleeper-agent-backdoor-results-are-messy) on LessWrong, finding that backdoor removal success depends heavily on implementation details like the optimizer used, whether CoT-distillation is employed, and the specific model architecture - sometimes with results opposite to the original paper's findings. This matters because it suggests that our understanding of backdoor persistence and removal in AI systems may be less reliable than previously thought, with significant implications for AI security research.

## Policy & Governance

[Georgetown CSET highlighted](https://cset.georgetown.edu/article/too-dangerous-to-release-is-becoming-ais-new-normal/) the growing trend of AI companies restricting access to their most capable models due to dual-use risks in cybersecurity and biological research, examining cases like GPT-Rosalind and Claude Mythos. The analysis explores broader questions about governance of access to powerful AI systems. This matters because it signals a potential shift toward more restrictive deployment practices that could become standard industry practice, influencing both innovation timelines and risk mitigation strategies.

The [European Commission held the third GPAI Signatory Taskforce meeting](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/third-gpai-signatory-taskforce-meeting-safety-and-security-chapter) focused on the Safety and Security chapter, continuing international coordination efforts on AI governance frameworks. This matters because it represents ongoing multilateral efforts to establish coordinated international standards for AI safety and security that could shape global AI development practices.
